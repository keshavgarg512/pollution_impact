Special Plants That Support Pollinators

Certain plants play a vital role in supporting pollinators by providing them with essential resources such as nectar, pollen, and shelter. Growing these plants in gardens and green spaces can create a haven for bees, butterflies, birds, and other pollinators, helping to sustain their populations.

Native Wildflowers

Native wildflowers are well-adapted to local climates and soil conditions, making them an excellent choice for pollinator-friendly gardens. They attract native pollinators, support biodiversity, and often require less water and maintenance.

  • Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta): A bright yellow flower that attracts bees and butterflies.
  • Milkweed (Asclepias spp.): Essential for monarch butterflies, which lay their eggs on milkweed plants.
  • Bee Balm (Monarda): A favorite of bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds, producing beautiful blooms in summer.

Herbaceous Plants

Herbaceous plants are non-woody plants that can provide significant nectar and pollen. Many herbaceous plants bloom throughout the growing season, offering a continuous food source for pollinators.

  • Lavender (Lavandula): Known for its fragrant flowers, lavender attracts bees and butterflies.
  • Sunflowers (Helianthus annuus): These large, bright flowers attract a variety of pollinators, including bees and birds.
  • Echinacea (Coneflower): Produces long-lasting flowers that attract bees and are rich in nectar.

Flowering Shrubs

Flowering shrubs provide pollinators with both food and habitat. They are an essential addition to any garden, offering shelter and nectar over long blooming seasons.

  • Butterfly Bush (Buddleja): Named for its ability to attract butterflies, this shrub has nectar-rich flowers.
  • Blueberry (Vaccinium): Aside from being a source of fruit, blueberries produce flowers that attract bees.
  • Spirea: This hardy shrub has clusters of small flowers that attract bees and butterflies.

Pollinator-Friendly Trees

Trees can be an excellent food source and shelter for pollinators. Some flowering trees provide a burst of blooms in the spring, attracting pollinators to their nectar-rich flowers.

  • Willow (Salix): A significant early-season food source for bees.
  • Maple (Acer): Provides nectar and pollen, especially in the early spring when other flowers are scarce.
  • Apple (Malus domestica): Apple blossoms attract bees, and they play a key role in the tree's pollination.

How You Can Help Pollinators Through Planting

Creating a garden with a variety of flowering plants, shrubs, and trees is one of the most effective ways to support pollinators. Here are some tips to help you design a pollinator-friendly garden:

  • Plant a Mix of Bloom Times: Choose plants that bloom at different times of the year to provide food for pollinators throughout the growing season.
  • Include Native Species: Native plants are more likely to attract local pollinators and thrive in your climate.
  • Avoid Pesticides: Many pesticides harm pollinators. Opt for natural pest control methods to keep your garden safe.
